The Privilege Waiver Cost

Privilege waiver can destroy your case and open the firm to liability

Bar disciplinary action, malpractice lawsuits, and client attrition follow quickly. Sharing privileged information with third parties — including AI vendors — can waive attorney-client privilege under most state bar rules. Most attorneys don't realize consumer AI platforms qualify as a third party.

ABA Model Rule 1.6 (confidentiality) explicitly covers this. Most bar associations prohibit using non-approved tools for client data. Yet associates and paralegals use consumer AI tools every day to speed up their work.

Real-World Scenario

The Waived Privilege

A late-night shortcut becomes a case-ending compliance event.

A junior associate is drafting a motion for summary judgment. To organize arguments quickly, they paste key deposition excerpts and attorney work product notes into ChatGPT. The AI processes it instantly. The associate submits the motion.

Months later, opposing counsel requests the firm's AI prompt history during discovery. The firm claims privilege — but the damage is done. The privileged information has been shared with a non-privileged third party, and the waiver argument is strong.

What follows

  • Privilege waiver motion filed by opposing counsel
  • Judge potentially grants access to all related communications
  • Client's case substantially weakened or lost
  • Malpractice lawsuit from client for damages caused
  • Bar disciplinary complaint against the associate and potentially the partner
